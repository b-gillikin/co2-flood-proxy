# Elicitation question bank (Stage 2)

Ask these conversationally, 1-2 at a time. Reorder based on what the user has already said. The point is to converge on a defensible thesis claim and contribution sentence for one PhD dissertation chapter — not to fill out a form.

The skill assumes PhD-level scope. Don't ask about level; ask about the chapter's specific shape.

## Opening

If the user hasn't said yet:

1. What is this chapter about, in one or two sentences?
2. Which chapter number is it in the dissertation, and what came before it (if anything)?
3. What's the rough timeline for finishing this chapter?

## Claim and contribution

4. What is the thesis claim you'd defend? Try to land on one sentence.
5. What's specifically novel — what hasn't been done that your chapter does?
6. What would it take for this claim to be falsified or seriously weakened?

If the claim sounds overstated, push back. Common overstatements: "I will predict X" when "I will characterize X" is actually defensible; "this transfers" when "I will test whether this transfers" is the actual contribution.

## Predecessor work

7. Is there predecessor work this chapter builds directly on — prior theses, papers on the same dataset, a research group's accumulated work?
8. If so, what specifically did each predecessor establish, and what is the chapter's *step beyond* it?

If there's no predecessor work, skip this section and treat the chapter as a standalone contribution. The skill handles both cases.

## Methodological frame

9. What methods are you using — anomaly detection, statistical modelling, qualitative analysis, mixed methods, simulation, fieldwork, archival, theoretical?
10. Specifically which techniques or methods, named?
11. Why these and not alternatives that the literature would point to first?

PhD chapters need to defend method choices at examination depth — establish "why this and not the alternatives the literature would point to first" as a positioned argument, not a default.

## Data and empirical scope

12. Is there empirical data involved? If so:
    - What data do you have in hand?
    - What needs to be acquired?
    - What's the data window — calendar dates?
    - Are there labels or ground-truth observations?
13. If the chapter is empirical but data is partial, what's blocking the rest?

## Transferability / portability / generalization

14. Does the chapter test whether something generalizes — across sites, across populations, across conditions, across time periods?
15. If yes: what's the calibration setting and what are the test settings? What's the criterion for "it transfers"?

If no transferability dimension, skip — many chapters don't have this and shouldn't be forced into it.

## Field positioning

16. What's the broader field this chapter speaks to? Name 2-3 active research conversations the chapter sits at the intersection of.
17. What's the current SOTA you're measuring against — for methods, for results, for framing?
18. Are there one or two recent papers that examiners would expect you to engage with directly?

## Examination depth

19. What's the methodological choice in the chapter that an examiner would probe hardest? Have a defensible answer ready.
20. What's the result direction you're betting on, and what does the chapter look like if that result doesn't materialize?

The second question is the most important one. A well-structured PhD chapter survives both outcomes — the kill-check pattern in the execution plan exists precisely to handle this.

## Logistical

21. What's your reference manager — Mendeley, Zotero, EndNote, Citavi, other?
22. What's the writing target — Word, LaTeX, Markdown-to-PDF?
23. Where should the chapter repo live — a path you can specify? (Stage 3 initializes it.)
24. Does this chapter thematically connect to your other chapters, or does it stand alone? (The skill doesn't sequence chapters — just records the connection so the scaffold's framing reflects it.)

## Sanity check before moving to Stage 3

Before generating the scaffold, the user should have given clear answers to at least:

- The thesis claim (in their own words, one sentence)
- The contribution (what's novel)
- The methodological frame
- Whether there's empirical data
- Whether there's a transferability dimension
- Whether there's predecessor work

If any of these is still vague, ask one more question. Don't proceed with a vague claim — the scaffold will reflect the vagueness back.
